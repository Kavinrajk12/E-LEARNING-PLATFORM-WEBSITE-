R2 Overview› Rate of Reaction
Rate of Reaction
R2.2 · Standard Level
Guiding Question

How can the rate of a reaction be controlled?

Definition

The rate of reaction is expressed as the change in concentration of a particular reactant or product per unit time.

Formula
Rate = Δ[concentration] / Δtime     (units: mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹)

Measuring Rate

Concentration changes in reactions are not usually measured directly. Common indirect methods include:

  • Gas volume or pressure — collected over time for gas-producing reactions
  • Mass loss — when a gas is evolved (e.g., CO₂ from CaCO₃ + HCl)
  • Colorimetry / absorbance — for reactions involving coloured species
  • Electrical conductivity — when ion concentrations change
  • Titration samples — quenched at intervals and titrated
Key skill
When time is the independent variable, you control time and measure how much has reacted. When time is the dependent variable, you measure the time for a fixed observable change (e.g., a colour change or precipitate formation).

Rate from Graphs

Rate at a given instant is found from the gradient (slope) of a concentration–time graph:

  • Average rate — gradient of a chord between two points
  • Instantaneous rate — gradient of the tangent at a specific time
Common error
Graphs of rate data can show systematic error (consistent offset from true value) and random error (scatter around the line). The shape of residuals helps distinguish them.
R2 Overview› Collision Theory
Collision Theory
R2.2 · Standard Level

Core Principle

Species react as a result of collisions of sufficient energy and proper orientation. Not every collision leads to a reaction.

⚡
Sufficient Energy
Colliding particles must meet or exceed the activation energy Eₐ.
🎯
Proper Orientation
Molecules must collide in the right geometry for bonds to break and form.
🔄
Collision Frequency
More frequent collisions generally increase the reaction rate.

Activation Energy (Eₐ)

Activation energy, Eₐ, is the minimum energy that colliding particles need for a successful collision leading to a reaction.

Definition

Eₐ is the energy difference between the reactants and the transition state (the highest-energy point on the reaction coordinate). It represents the energy barrier that must be overcome.

Link to Kinetic Molecular Theory

Collision theory is an extension of kinetic molecular theory. Particles in a gas or liquid are in constant random motion. The distribution of kinetic energies is described by the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. Only the fraction of particles with energy ≥ Eₐ can react upon collision.

Exam tip
Always link collision theory to Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions when explaining how temperature increases rate — it's more precise than saying "particles move faster."
R2 Overview› Factors Affecting Rate
Factors Affecting Rate
R2.2 · Standard Level

Key Variables

Factors that influence the rate of a reaction include pressure/concentration, surface area, temperature, and the presence of a catalyst.

FactorEffect on RateReason
↑ Concentration / Pressure Rate increases More particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions
↑ Surface Area Rate increases More exposed particles → more collisions possible
↑ Temperature Rate increases significantly Greater fraction of particles exceed Eₐ; more frequent collisions
Adding a Catalyst Rate increases Alternative pathway with lower Eₐ
Controlling Variables
When investigating the effect of ONE factor on rate, all other variables must be controlled. For example, when testing the effect of concentration, temperature and surface area must remain constant.

Temperature — A Special Case

A small increase in temperature causes a disproportionately large increase in rate. This is because the number of particles with energy ≥ Eₐ increases exponentially with temperature (related to the Arrhenius equation at AHL).

Rule of thumb
A 10 K rise in temperature approximately doubles the reaction rate for many reactions (though this depends on Eₐ).
R2 Overview› Maxwell-Boltzmann
Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution
R2.2 · Standard Level

What is the Maxwell-Boltzmann Curve?

The Maxwell-Boltzmann energy distribution curve shows the distribution of kinetic energies among particles in a sample at a given temperature. The x-axis represents kinetic energy; the y-axis represents the number of particles (or probability).

📈
Key Features
Starts at zero, rises to a peak (most probable energy), then tails off asymptotically.
🌡️
Effect of Temperature
Higher T → curve flattens and shifts right; peak lowers; more particles exceed Eₐ.
⚡
Eₐ on the Curve
Area under curve to the right of Eₐ = fraction of particles that can react.

Effect of Temperature

When temperature increases:

  • The peak shifts to the right (higher most probable energy)
  • The peak lowers (total area under curve stays the same)
  • The curve broadens
  • A significantly larger fraction of particles have energy ≥ Eₐ

Effect of Eₐ on Reaction Rate

For two reactions at the same temperature, a reaction with a lower Eₐ will have a greater fraction of particles that can react — so it proceeds faster. The Maxwell-Boltzmann curve lets you compare these fractions visually.

Effect of a Catalyst

A catalyst lowers Eₐ — on the Maxwell-Boltzmann curve, you can show this by moving the Eₐ marker to the left, revealing that a much larger fraction of particles now have sufficient energy to react, even at the same temperature.

R2 Overview› Catalysts
Catalysts & Energy Profiles
R2.2 · Standard Level

How Catalysts Work

Catalysts increase the rate of reaction by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy, Eₐ.

  • They are not consumed in the overall reaction
  • They affect both forward and reverse reactions equally — equilibrium position does not change
  • They do not change the enthalpy change (ΔH) of the reaction
Equilibrium link
The catalyst increases the rate of BOTH the forward and reverse reactions by the same factor. This means equilibrium is reached faster, but the equilibrium position (Kc) is unchanged.

Energy Profiles

An energy profile (reaction coordinate diagram) shows energy on the y-axis and reaction progress on the x-axis. You need to be able to sketch and explain these:

FeatureExothermicEndothermic
Products vs. Reactants Products are lower in energy Products are higher in energy
ΔH Negative (−) Positive (+)
Eₐ (forward) Smaller than Eₐ (reverse) Larger than Eₐ (reverse)

Catalyst on an Energy Profile

With a catalyst, the energy profile shows a lower activation energy hump. The positions of reactants and products (and therefore ΔH) remain unchanged — only the transition state is lowered.

Types of catalysts
Homogeneous catalysts are in the same phase as the reactants. Heterogeneous catalysts are in a different phase (often solid catalysts with gaseous/liquid reactants). Transition metals are particularly useful as heterogeneous catalysts due to their variable oxidation states and ability to adsorb reactants.

Rate Expression & Order of Reaction
R2.2 AHL · Higher Level Only
⚠ AHL Content
This section is required for Higher Level students only.
Guiding Question

What is the relationship between the rate equation and the mechanism of a chemical reaction?

The Rate Equation

For a reaction A + B → products, the rate equation (rate expression) takes the form:

Rate equation
rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ

Where k is the rate constant, m is the order with respect to A, and n is the order with respect to B.

Critical point
Rate equations can ONLY be determined experimentally — they cannot be deduced from the balanced stoichiometric equation.

Order of Reaction

The order with respect to a reactant is the exponent to which its concentration is raised in the rate equation. It describes how many particles of that reactant take part in the rate-determining step.

OrderRate ∝Meaning
Zero (0)constantRate independent of [A]; A not in rate-determining step
First (1)[A]Rate doubles when [A] doubles
Second (2)[A]²Rate quadruples when [A] doubles

The overall reaction order is the sum of all individual orders: m + n.

Units of the Rate Constant k

The units of k depend on the overall reaction order. They must make the rate equation dimensionally consistent (rate has units mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹).

Second order
mol⁻¹ dm³ s⁻¹
Third order

R2 Overview› Reaction Mechanism AHL
Reaction Mechanism
R2.2 AHL · Higher Level Only

Elementary Steps

Many reactions occur in a series of elementary steps. The overall reaction is the sum of these steps. The slowest step determines the rate of the overall reaction — this is the rate-determining step (RDS).

🐢
Rate-Determining Step
The slowest elementary step; it acts as the bottleneck for the whole reaction.
🔀
Intermediate
A species formed and consumed during the mechanism; does NOT appear in the overall equation.
⚡
Transition State
The highest-energy point on an energy profile; a transient species that cannot be isolated.

Intermediates vs. Transition States

IntermediateTransition State
On energy profileA trough (local minimum)A peak (local maximum)
Can be isolated?Yes (in principle)No
LifetimeFinite (brief)Effectively zero

Molecularity

The molecularity of an elementary step is the number of reacting particles involved:

Unimolecular
1 particle
e.g., A → products
Bimolecular
2 particles
e.g., A + B → products
Termolecular
3 particles
Rare; requires 3 simultaneous collisions
Note
—
Molecularity applies to elementary steps only, not overall equations

Evaluating Mechanisms

A proposed mechanism is consistent if:

  1. The elementary steps sum to give the overall equation
  2. The species in the slow step match the reactants in the rate equation
  3. All intermediates cancel out in the sum
Important caveat
Mechanisms are only considered "possible" — they can never be proven with absolute certainty from kinetic data alone.
R2 Overview› Order of Reaction AHL
Deducing Order from Data
R2.2 AHL · Higher Level Only

Using Experimental Data

To deduce the order with respect to each reactant, compare experiments where only one concentration is changed at a time ("initial rates method").

📋 Worked Example

Given the data below for A + B → products:

Exp.[A] / mol dm⁻³[B] / mol dm⁻³Rate / mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
10.100.102.0 × 10⁻³
20.200.104.0 × 10⁻³
30.100.208.0 × 10⁻³

Step 1: Compare Exp 1 & 2 (only [A] changes): rate doubles when [A] doubles → order = 1 with respect to A.

Step 2: Compare Exp 1 & 3 (only [B] changes): rate quadruples when [B] doubles → order = 2 with respect to B.

Result: rate = k[A][B]², overall order = 3.

Half-life (t½)

The half-life is the time for the concentration of a reactant to fall to half its initial value.

OrderHalf-life relationship
Zerot½ decreases as reaction proceeds
Firstt½ is constant (independent of [A])
Secondt½ increases as reaction proceeds
First-order recognition
A constant half-life is the hallmark of a first-order reaction. If successive half-lives are equal on a concentration–time graph, the reaction is first order.
R2 Overview› Rate Constant k AHL
The Rate Constant, k
R2.2 AHL · Higher Level Only

Properties of k

The rate constant k is a proportionality constant in the rate equation. Its key properties:

  • k is temperature dependent — it increases with temperature
  • k is specific to a reaction at a given temperature
  • k is not affected by concentration changes
  • The units of k depend on the overall order of the reaction
Temperature dependence

The relationship between k and temperature is quantified by the Arrhenius equation (see next section). As T increases, k increases, so rate increases — even at constant concentration.

Solving Problems with the Rate Equation

🧮 Practice Problem

For the reaction: rate = k[NO]²[O₂], the rate is 2.4 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹ when [NO] = 0.020 mol dm⁻³ and [O₂] = 0.010 mol dm⁻³.

Calculate k and give its units.

Solution: k = rate / ([NO]²[O₂]) = 2.4 × 10⁻³ / (0.020² × 0.010) = 2.4 × 10⁻³ / (4 × 10⁻⁶) = 600 mol⁻² dm⁶ s⁻¹

Energy Profiles for Multi-Step Reactions

For a reaction with multiple elementary steps, the energy profile shows multiple peaks (one for each transition state) and troughs (one for each intermediate). The rate-determining step corresponds to the largest energy barrier (highest activation energy).

The Arrhenius Factor, A

The Arrhenius factor (pre-exponential factor), A, takes into account:

  • The frequency of collisions
  • The fraction with proper orientation (steric factor)

The units of A are the same as the units of k (determined by the overall order of reaction).

Qualitative Relationship

As temperature increases, the exponential term e^(−Eₐ/RT) increases — meaning a greater fraction of collisions have energy ≥ Eₐ. This results in a larger k and therefore a faster rate.

Key relationship
Higher T → larger e^(−Eₐ/RT) → larger k → faster rate. For reactions with high Eₐ, k is more sensitive to temperature changes.
R2 Overview› Arrhenius — Graphical Form AHL
Linear Form of the Arrhenius Equation
R2.2 AHL · Higher Level Only

Linearisation

Taking the natural logarithm of both sides of k = Ae^(−Eₐ/RT):

Linear Arrhenius form
ln k = −(Eₐ/R) · (1/T) + ln A

This is in the form y = mx + c where:

  • y = ln k
  • x = 1/T
  • slope (m) = −Eₐ/R
  • y-intercept (c) = ln A

Using the Arrhenius Graph

Plot ln k (y-axis) against 1/T (x-axis):

  • The graph is a straight line with negative slope
  • Eₐ = −slope × R
  • A = e^(y-intercept)
📋 Worked Example

A plot of ln k vs. 1/T gives a slope of −9600 K. Calculate Eₐ.

Solution: Eₐ = −slope × R = −(−9600) × 8.314 = 79,814 J mol⁻¹ ≈ 79.8 kJ mol⁻¹

Two-Temperature Form

If you have k at two temperatures, you can use:

Two-point Arrhenius
ln(k₂/k₁) = −(Eₐ/R) · (1/T₂ − 1/T₁)
Units check
Always convert temperature to kelvin. Eₐ will come out in J mol⁻¹ (using R = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹) — convert to kJ mol⁻¹ for a cleaner answer.
